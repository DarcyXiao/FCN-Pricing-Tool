FCN Pricing Simulation — Documentation

Methodology, mathematics, and implementation details

1. Overview

This tool prices Fixed Coupon Notes (FCNs) using Monte Carlo simulation with correlated Geometric Brownian Motion (GBM). It supports two calculation modes:

  • Fair Value Mode — Given a coupon rate, compute the present value of the FCN.
  • Fair Coupon Mode — Given a target fair value (default 100% of notional), solve for the equilibrium coupon rate using the bisection (binary search) method.

2. Data Preparation

Historical closing prices are fetched from Yahoo Finance via a local Flask backend. From the price series we compute:

$$r_t = \ln\!\left(\frac{P_t}{P_{t-1}}\right) \qquad \sigma = \operatorname{std}(r) \times \sqrt{252} \qquad \rho_{ij} = \frac{\operatorname{Cov}(r_i, r_j)}{\sigma_i \cdot \sigma_j}$$
SymbolMeaningComputation
$r_t$Daily log return$\ln(P_t / P_{t-1})$
$\sigma$Annualized volatility$\operatorname{std}(r_{\text{daily}}) \times \sqrt{252}$
$\rho_{ij}$Correlation between assets $i$ and $j$$\operatorname{Cov}(r_i, r_j) \,/\, (\sigma_i^{\text{daily}} \cdot \sigma_j^{\text{daily}})$

3. Monte Carlo Simulation — Correlated GBM

Under the risk-neutral measure, each asset price $S_i$ follows a stochastic differential equation:

$$\frac{dS_i}{S_i} = r \cdot dt + \sigma_i \cdot dW_i$$

where $r$ is the risk-free rate, $\sigma_i$ is the annualized volatility, and $dW_i$ are correlated Brownian motions with $\operatorname{Corr}(dW_i, dW_j) = \rho_{ij}$.

3.1 Cholesky Decomposition

To simulate correlated random shocks, we factor the correlation matrix $\Sigma$ using Cholesky decomposition:

$$\begin{aligned} \Sigma &= L \cdot L^T &&\text{(Cholesky factorization of the correlation matrix)} \\[4pt] Z_{\text{corr}} &= L \cdot Z &&(Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)\; \text{i.i.d. — Box-Muller transform}) \\[4pt] S_i(t+dt) &= S_i(t) \cdot \exp\!\Bigl((r - \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma_i^2)\,dt + \sigma_i\sqrt{dt} \cdot Z_{\text{corr}}[i]\Bigr) \end{aligned}$$

The Box-Muller transform converts uniform random numbers to standard normals:

$$Z = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1} \cdot \cos(2\pi U_2), \quad U_1, U_2 \sim \mathcal{U}(0,1)$$

3.2 Discretization

Paths are simulated at daily granularity with $dt = 1/252$ years per step. A product with $M$ months of tenor uses $21 \times M + 5$ total steps to account for calendar effects.

4. Payoff Structure & Discounting

The FCN is a worst-of basket product. All barrier conditions are evaluated on the minimum return across all assets. Three mutually exclusive scenarios determine the payoff:

ScenarioConditionDiscounted Payoff
① Knock-Out (KO) All assets $\ge$ KO barrier on any day $t \ge$ KO Start $100 \cdot \bigl(1 + c \cdot \frac{T-5}{252}\bigr) \cdot e^{-r \cdot T/252}$
② Safe Maturity No KO occurred; either no KI, or KI but worst $\ge$ Strike at maturity $100 \cdot \bigl(1 + c \cdot \frac{T_{\text{total}}-5}{252}\bigr) \cdot e^{-r \cdot T_{\text{total}}/252}$
③ Delivery KI breached AND worst asset $\lt$ Strike at maturity $\bigl(100\,c\,\frac{T_{\text{total}}-5}{252} + 100\,\frac{S_{\text{worst}}}{\text{Strike}}\bigr) \cdot e^{-r \cdot T_{\text{total}}/252}$
KI Types:
American (Continuous) — KI is observed every day. Any breach triggers KI permanently.
European (At Maturity) — KI is observed only on the final day.

5. Fair Value Calculation

The fair value is the expected discounted payoff across all Monte Carlo paths:

$$\text{Fair Value (\%)} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^{N} PV_k$$

where $N$ is the number of simulated paths, and $PV_k$ is the present value of path $k$ discounted at the risk-free rate using continuous compounding. The standard error of the estimate is:

$$\text{SE} \approx \frac{\operatorname{std}(PV)}{\sqrt{N}}$$

6. Fair Coupon Solver — Bisection Method

The fair coupon $c^*$ is defined as the rate that makes Fair Value equal to the Target Value (typically 100%):

$$c^* = \underset{c}{\operatorname{arg\,min}}\; \bigl|\, FV(c) - \text{Target} \,\bigr|$$

Since $FV(c)$ is monotonically increasing in $c$ (higher coupon → higher present value), we can use the bisection (binary search) algorithm:

  1. Set initial bounds: $c_{\text{low}} = 0.1\%$, $c_{\text{high}} = 50\%$
  2. Verify: $FV(c_{\text{low}}) \lt \text{Target} \lt FV(c_{\text{high}})$. If not, expand or report that the target is unreachable.
  3. Compute midpoint: $c_{\text{mid}} = \frac{c_{\text{low}} + c_{\text{high}}}{2}$
  4. Run Monte Carlo simulation with coupon $c_{\text{mid}}$ → get $FV(c_{\text{mid}})$
  5. If $FV(c_{\text{mid}}) \gt \text{Target}$ → coupon too high: $c_{\text{high}} = c_{\text{mid}}$
  6. If $FV(c_{\text{mid}}) \lt \text{Target}$ → coupon too low: $c_{\text{low}} = c_{\text{mid}}$
  7. Repeat until $|FV - \text{Target}| \lt 0.05$ or $(c_{\text{high}} - c_{\text{low}}) \lt 0.01\%$ or max 25 iterations
  8. Return $\displaystyle \frac{c_{\text{low}} + c_{\text{high}}}{2}$ as the fair coupon

Convergence rate: $O\bigl(\log_2(\frac{\text{initial range}}{\text{tolerance}})\bigr)$. With range [0.1%, 50%] and 0.01% coupon precision, at most:

$$\lceil \log_2(4990) \rceil \approx 13 \text{ iterations}$$

7. Key Assumptions & Limitations

  • Constant volatility — GBM assumes constant $\sigma$; real markets exhibit volatility clustering and regime changes.
  • Constant correlation — correlations are computed from historical data and assumed constant; they can spike during market stress.
  • No dividends — Yahoo Finance adjusted-close prices are used; explicit dividend yields are not modeled.
  • No jump risk — plain GBM without Merton jump-diffusion; tail risk may be underestimated.
  • Constant risk-free rate — yield curve effects are ignored.
  • Daily monitoring — continuous barrier observation is approximated at daily frequency (~21 steps/month).
  • Monte Carlo noise — sampling error $\sim O(1/\sqrt{N})$. With $N = 2000$ paths, the standard error on probability estimates is ~2 percentage points.
